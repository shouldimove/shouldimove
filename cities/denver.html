Is Denver an Affordable Place to Live?

Denver's cost of living index is 127 — about 27% above the U.S. average and slightly above Austin. Colorado charges a flat 4.4% state income tax, eating into part of the headline salary advantage versus no-income-tax states like Texas, Tennessee, or Florida. Median 1BR rent runs around $1,800. The financial case is strongest for people moving from genuinely expensive cities (NYC, SF, Boston, Seattle); for people moving from Chicago, Minneapolis, or most of the Midwest, the move is roughly a wash or slightly negative on paper.

For reference: $90,000 in Denver delivers roughly the same purchasing power as $141,393 would in New York City, after adjusting for both cost of living and state/city income taxes.

Who Should Move to Denver?

Good fit for:

Outdoor-lifestyle people (skiing, hiking, mountain biking — all within an hour), tech and aerospace workers (lockheed, northrop, growing startup scene), remote workers wanting four real seasons, anyone moving from california who wants similar climate features at lower cost.

Less of a fit for:

People allergic to altitude (5,280 ft is genuinely felt for the first few weeks and during exercise), anyone hoping for texas-level prices, people who hate dry air and chapped lips, those expecting the 'cheap' denver of 2015 — it hasn't been that for years.

Frequently Asked Questions About Moving to Denver

Is Denver actually affordable?

Not particularly, by 2026 standards. The cost of living index is 127, which puts it roughly even with Austin and well above the U.S. average. The 'affordable Denver' reputation lingers from a decade ago — prices have caught up. It's still cheaper than San Francisco, Seattle, or New York, but more expensive than Phoenix, Nashville, or most Midwest cities.

What's the income tax rate in Colorado?

Colorado has a flat 4.4% state income tax that applies to all income levels. Denver does not add a city income tax. Compared to high-tax states like California (top rate over 13%) or New York City (around 10.4% combined), Colorado is moderate; compared to no-tax states like Texas, Florida, or Tennessee, you're giving up about 4–5% of your gross income.

Does the altitude actually matter?

Yes, especially for the first 1–4 weeks if you're not used to it. Denver sits at 5,280 feet, where the air has about 17% less oxygen than at sea level. Most people adjust within a month but exercise capacity is reduced for longer, and dehydration is easier. Skiing the mountains (8,000–12,000 ft) is a different conversation — even acclimated Denver residents feel it up there.

Is Denver good for remote work?

Reasonably so. The tech infrastructure is solid, the coworking and meetup scene is strong, and the time zone (Mountain) splits the difference between Pacific and Eastern. The Colorado flat tax is the cost of admission compared to no-tax states. The biggest reason remote workers pick Denver isn't financial — it's the outdoor lifestyle. If that's not your thing, Austin or Nashville are almost certainly better financial choices.

How does Denver compare to Salt Lake City?

Salt Lake City has a similar outdoor-lifestyle pitch with a lower cost of living (COL around 107 vs Denver's 127) and a 4.65% flat state income tax. SLC is more religiously conservative, has weaker bar/restaurant culture in many people's view, and a smaller tech scene — but it's noticeably cheaper. For pure financial purposes with similar mountain access, SLC often wins; for nightlife and a more diverse social scene, Denver does.
